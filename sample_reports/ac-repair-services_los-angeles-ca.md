## 2. Market Attractiveness

- **Top 2 demand drivers:**

- **Consolidation landscape:** Highly fragmented (80–120 competitors, top 10 <25% share) but PE-active (27 firms acquiring HVAC in 2026; Blackstone paid 18.5× EBITDA for Champions Group)
- **Key market risk:** PE consolidation inflating entry multiples (5–7× becoming 8–12×) and compressing independent operator margins through national platform competition

## 3. Competitive Position
**Competitive intensity is HIGH** due to fragmentation, low switching costs, and Google LSA bidding wars ($300–500 CAC in LA vs. $150–250 national average). The business is defensible only through maintenance contract stickiness (30%+ recurring revenue creates 80%+ retention) and replacement sales capture (15%+ attachment rate on service calls). Without these, the operator is commoditized and margin-trapped. **Barrier height: MODERATE** — 6–18 month licensing timeline (EPA 608, C-20) and 12–24 month brand-building create modest entry friction, but experienced technicians can launch competing operations for $150K–$400K. **Biggest competitive threat:** PE-backed consolidators (Champions Group, national platforms) offering volume discounts to property managers and compressing pricing on maintenance contracts.

## 4. Unit Economics
- **Normalized EBITDA margin:** 15–22% (PE basis, post owner add-back) — **upside case 20–22% requires 35%+ maintenance revenue, 15%+ replacement attachment, 55%+ owned channels; downside 12–15% if transactional-heavy**
- **Revenue per technician:** $180K–$250K annually (assumes 1,200–1,500 billable hours at $150–$175 blended rate; top quartile operators achieve $250K+ through maintenance contract leverage)
- **Revenue quality:** 
  - Recurring revenue: 25–40% estimated (30%+ required for PE-quality; <25% indicates commoditization)
  - Customer concentration: Property management contracts create 15–30% revenue concentration risk if top 3 accounts churn post-acquisition
- **Key profitability risk:** Revenue mix skew toward transactional emergency calls (<70% transactional, <20% maintenance) compresses EBITDA to 10–12% and creates high CAC dependency on Google LSA/paid channels

## 5. Operational Profile
- **Business complexity: MODERATE-TO-HIGH** — Field service delivery with W-2 technician workforce (CA AB5 compliance risk if using 1099s), EPA 608/C-20 licensing requirements, multi-channel lead generation (Google LSA, Yelp, owned repeat/referral), and mixed revenue model (maintenance contracts, transactional repair, equipment replacement) requiring distinct sales motions and margin management.
- **Key person dependency:** **HIGH RISK** — Owner typically holds primary customer relationships (especially property management contracts), C-20 contractor license (required for equipment replacement; 4–6 year apprenticeship to replace), EPA 608 Universal certification, vendor relationships, and pricing discipline. Owner departure creates 6–12 month customer churn risk and $200K–$400K replacement cost (GM salary + technician certifications + potential revenue loss).
- **Primary CapEx requirement:** $80K–$150K per service truck + tools; fleet replacement cycle 5–7 years; working capital for payroll/inventory $50K–$150K per truck
- **Technology maturity:** Partially automated — Field service management software (ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro) for dispatch/scheduling, but customer acquisition remains manual (phone/web leads) and technician productivity dependent on individual skill/experience
